# User Awareness Guide — Recognising and Reporting Phishing

**Document Reference:** UA-001  
**Audience:** All Staff  
**Classification:** Internal — General Distribution

---

## Introduction

Phishing emails are one of the most common ways that attackers attempt to compromise organisations. This guide helps you recognise suspicious emails, understand why they are dangerous, and know exactly what to do if you receive one.

You do not need to be a technical expert to protect the organisation — your vigilance is one of our most important security controls.

---

## What Is Phishing?

Phishing is when an attacker sends a fraudulent email designed to trick you into:

- Clicking a malicious link (which may steal your login credentials or install malware)
- Opening a dangerous attachment (which may install malware on your device)
- Transferring money or sharing sensitive information (business email compromise)

Attackers often disguise these emails to look like they come from trusted sources — colleagues, banks, IT support, couriers, or well-known companies.

---

## Warning Signs to Look For

### Sender
- The display name looks familiar, but the actual email address is unusual or misspelt
- The domain does not match the organisation it claims to be from (e.g., `support@micros0ft-help.com`)
- You were not expecting an email from this person or organisation

### Content
- Creates urgency or panic: *"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours"*
- Asks you to verify your login credentials or payment details
- Contains unexpected links or attachments
- Poor spelling, grammar, or unusual formatting
- Generic greeting (e.g., "Dear Customer") rather than your name

### Links
- Hover over any link before clicking — does the destination URL match what you expect?
- URLs that use lookalike domains (e.g., `paypa1.com`, `support-microsoft.net`)
- Links that redirect through URL shorteners

### Attachments
- Unexpected attachments, even from known senders
- Files with double extensions (e.g., `invoice.pdf.exe`)
- Office documents asking you to "Enable Macros" or "Enable Editing"

## What To Do If You Receive a Suspicious Email

**Do:**
✅ Report it to the security team using [reporting method — e.g., the "Report Phishing" button / forwarding to security@[organisation]]  
✅ Leave the email in your inbox (do not delete it — the security team may need to investigate)  
✅ Tell your manager if you think others in your team may have received the same email

**Do not:**
❌ Click any links in the email  
❌ Open any attachments  
❌ Reply to the email  
❌ Forward it to colleagues to "warn them" (this spreads the risk)

---

## What To Do If You Already Clicked Something

**Do not panic.** Contact the IT Security team immediately via [contact method — e.g., phone number / Teams message / helpdesk ticket].

Tell them:
- What you clicked or opened
- When it happened
- What happened next (did a website load? Did a document open? Did you enter any information?)

The sooner you report it, the sooner the team can act to protect your account and our systems.

---

## Remember

- **When in doubt, don't click.** Report it instead.
- **It is never wrong to report.** Even if the email turns out to be legitimate, we would rather receive ten false alarms than miss one real attack.
- **Attackers target everyone.** Phishing is not a sign that you did something wrong — it is a sign that the organisation is being targeted, and your awareness is our best defence.
